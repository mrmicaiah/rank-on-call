## Critical Issues — the headline findings

**Sample Company A (the neglected one):**
- **The website tells every visitor the business has done zero jobs.** Three "stats" counters on the homepage are broken and all render as **"0 +"** — literally "0+ Gutter Installs, 0+ Gutter Repairs, 0+ 5-Star Reviews." Meanwhile the top of the same page claims "70+ reviews." A potential customer sees a company advertising that it has completed nothing.
- **Three different business addresses across its own listings** — three different street addresses in three different ZIP codes, spread across the website, one directory, and another review platform. Google can't confirm where this business actually is.
- **The business can't decide what it does.** The logo and one social profile say "gutters & roofing"; the web address and page title say gutters only; a second social profile uses a *different* handle again. Google is told three different stories and commits to none.

**Sample Company B (the polished one):**
- **Not on the first page of Google for its own primary search, in its own city.** Despite a 12-year-old web address, a modern site, and 240+ reviews, this business does **not** appear in the top 10 organic results for the main "[service] [their city]" search. The top spots go to a national franchise, an aggregator, and several local competitors. *(Note: this is organic search only — the business may appear in the Google map/local pack, which this report doesn't measure. But not appearing in the main organic results is a real, fixable gap.)*
- **Three different founding years on its own materials.** The site's own metadata says "since 2005," the body copy says "since 2003" and "two decades," and third-party directories list "Est. 2004." Small, but it's the kind of inconsistency that erodes the trust signals Google (and customers) rely on.
- **The web address quietly expires in under 5 months.** Easy to miss, expensive to forget — a lapsed domain takes the whole site (and every ranking earned over 12 years) down with it.
